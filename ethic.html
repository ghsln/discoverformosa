A Deeper Dive into Taiwanese Etiquette

Understanding the "why" behind these customs will help you navigate social situations with confidence and show respect for the local culture.

Cultural Insight: Taiwanese culture places a strong emphasis on harmony, respect, and collective well-being. These etiquette guidelines reflect values that have been shaped by Confucian principles and centuries of tradition.

Greetings

While handshakes are common in business, a slight, graceful nod of the head or a very subtle bow is a traditional and deeply respected form of greeting. This gesture signifies humility and respect for the other person.

In Practice

You don't need to perform a deep bow. A meaningful nod while making eye contact and offering a verbal greeting like "Nǐ hǎo" (Hello) is perfectly appropriate. The key is the intention behind it, showing that you are acknowledging the other person with respect.

Cultural Significance

This custom reflects the Confucian value of humility and the importance of showing respect to others, particularly elders and those in positions of authority.

Do

  • Use a slight nod or subtle bow
  • Make eye contact while greeting
  • Use appropriate verbal greetings
  • Show humility and respect

Don't

  • Offer overly familiar physical contact
  • Ignore proper greeting protocols with elders
  • Make exaggerated or deep bows
  • Forget to acknowledge people properly

Shoes Off

This custom is rooted in practicality and hygiene. The outside world is considered "dirty," and the inside of a home is a clean, protected space. Removing your shoes before entering is a way to keep this boundary intact.

In Practice

This is non-negotiable in homes and also applies to many traditional inns (minsu), temples, and even some doctor's offices or spas. You will typically see a pile of shoes at the entrance or a designated shoe cabinet. It's a good idea to wear socks without holes or easily removable shoes. The host will often provide slippers for you to wear inside.

Where It Applies

Private homes, traditional inns (minsu), temples, some restaurants with tatami seating, certain clinics and spas, and sometimes even in modern apartments.

Pro Tip

When visiting someone's home, observe what others are doing. If you see shoes at the entrance, follow suit. It's better to ask if you're unsure rather than assume it's okay to keep your shoes on.

Attitude in Temples

Temples are active places of worship, not just tourist attractions. Dressing modestly (covering shoulders and knees) shows respect for the deities and the devotees. Speaking quietly maintains the serene atmosphere necessary for prayer and meditation.

Photography Etiquette

Before taking photos, always look for signs or observe if locals are doing so. Never use a flash on religious statues or artifacts, as it can be damaging and disruptive.

Respectful Conduct

Avoid pointing your feet at altars or statues while sitting, as feet are considered the lowest and dirtiest part of the body. Maintain a quiet demeanor and observe how locals behave.

Do

  • Dress modestly (cover shoulders and knees)
  • Speak quietly and maintain a respectful demeanor
  • Observe photography rules and restrictions
  • Follow the lead of local worshippers

Don't

  • Use flash photography
  • Touch religious artifacts without permission
  • Point your feet at altars or statues
  • Disturb people who are praying or meditating

Gift Giving

Presenting or receiving anything, a gift, a business card, a purchase, even change, with both hands is a sign of full attention, sincerity, and respect. It shows you are honoring the transaction and the person you are interacting with.

In Practice

The recipient will likely also use both hands. They may not open the gift immediately, as this is done to avoid appearing greedy or being embarrassed if the gift is not what they expected. When paying for items in a store, it is polite to place the money on the small tray provided rather than handing it directly to the cashier.

Gift Opening Customs

Don't be offended if your gift isn't opened immediately. This is a common practice to show that the relationship is more important than the gift itself, and to avoid potential embarrassment for both giver and receiver.

Pro Tip

When presenting a business card, hold it with both hands with the text facing the recipient so they can read it easily. Take a moment to look at the card you receive before putting it away.

Chopsticks

Sticking chopsticks upright in a bowl of rice directly resembles the incense sticks burned for the dead in Buddhist and Taoist funeral rites. It is considered a terrible omen, symbolizing death and inviting bad luck.

In Practice

If you need to set your chopsticks down, always place them across the rim of your bowl or on the provided chopstick rest. Never spear your food with them or use them to point at people.

Additional Taboos

Avoid passing food from your chopsticks directly to someone else's, as this also resembles a funeral ritual. Don't tap your chopsticks on bowls or plates, and don't use them to move bowls or plates around the table.

Do

  • Place chopsticks on the rest or bowl rim
  • Use serving utensils for shared dishes
  • Hold chopsticks properly
  • Ask for help if you're unfamiliar with usage

Don't

  • Stick chopsticks upright in food
  • Spear food with chopsticks
  • Use them to gesture or point at people
  • Pass food directly from your chopsticks to others

Face Culture

"Face" represents a person's reputation, dignity, and prestige. Causing someone to "lose face" through public criticism, anger, or direct confrontation is one of the most serious social faux pas. The goal is to maintain harmony and mutual respect at all times.

In Practice

If you have a disagreement or complaint, address it calmly and privately. Be indirect and diplomatic. Taiwanese communication is often subtle, so pay attention to tone and non-verbal cues. A person may say "maybe" or "it might be difficult" when they actually mean "no."

Giving Face

You can "give face" by showing respect, offering sincere compliments (without exaggeration), and acknowledging someone's status or achievements appropriately. This helps build strong relationships.

Pro Tip

When making requests or giving feedback, use indirect language. Instead of "This is wrong," try "Perhaps we could consider another approach." Pay attention to subtle cues like hesitation or avoidance of direct answers, which might indicate discomfort with your request.

Queueing

This is a matter of collective efficiency and fairness. Pushing or cutting in line is seen as extremely selfish and disrespectful to everyone who is waiting their turn.

In Practice

You will see orderly lines for everything: the MRT, buses, night market stalls, and ticket counters. Always look for the end of the line. On escalators, especially in Taipei, the unwritten rule is to stand on the right and leave the left side clear for people who are in a hurry to walk up.

Public Transportation

When using public transportation, wait for passengers to exit before boarding. Offer seats to elderly, pregnant, or disabled passengers. Keep your voice down and avoid loud conversations on phones.

Do

  • Form orderly lines and wait your turn
  • Stand on the right side of escalators
  • Let people exit before boarding transportation
  • Be patient and respectful in queues

Don't

  • Cut in line or push ahead
  • Block the left side of escalators
  • Board before others have exited
  • Become impatient or complain loudly
